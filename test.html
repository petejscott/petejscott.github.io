Places to Intervene in a System

5:09 AM Saturday, September 12, 2015

The order of this list defines the most effective to least effective places to alter the way a system works. Changes to a system should take place closer to the lower numbers (ideally, #1).

This is a summary of Donella Meadow's "Places to Intervene in a System", which I've attempted to more narrowly focus on a system in place at a software development company. Donella Meadow wrote these in the opposite order, which I found silly until I wrote them in this order and discovered that this order is silly. I highly recommend the original article, though it should be noted that it was expanded from 9 to 12 places (as noted in the wikipedia article, Twelve Leverage Points)

1. The mindset or paradigm out of which the goals, rules, feedback structure arise: This is the most fundamental level at which change can be affected. If the mindset of the company is focused on short-term revenue at the cost of long-term revenue, or exceeding the safe capacity of its employees, or simply one of lackadaisicalness, then the goals of the system will be ill-defined, absent, unattainable, or contradictory. If the mindset and the goals are insufficient, the system's components cannot self-organize (they lack the direction and cohesion necessary), and the rules of the system will reward/punish incorrect decisions and behaviors, and introduce constraints (exhausted or lazy employees). Essentially, without this, the ability to intervene anywhere in the system is comprised.

2. The goals of the system: If the mindset or paradigm is correct, the goals are the next focal point. A good goal (perhaps following Lean Manufacturing guidelines: specific, measurable, attainable, realistic, time-based) can drive employees to self-organize around a commonly understood and valued goal. If the goal is non-existent or poor (e.g. "Make a difference"), the self-organization will be stifled by contradictory approaches (goal is not specific), uncertainty and constantly changing directions (not measurable), demotivation (not attainable), defeat (not realistic), or laziness/lack of focus (not time-based). The employees will not operate with a singular purpose, which cripples the ability to self-organize and consequently, cripples the ability to drive the lower levels of the system toward the desired outcome.

3. The power of self-organization: A self-organizing group can control the rules of the system, inspect the information, and apply that information to positive and negative feedback loops in order to attain the goal. Without self-organization, individuals will derive their own rules, utilize different information sets, and maintain their own sets of feedback loops (or none at all). The lack of self-organization has the very real capability to cripple the rest of the system, and (to reinforce the above), self-organization becomes improbably (if not impossible) without well-defined goals around which to organize. A self-organizing team can direct itself and improve itself. A collection of independent developers requires constant direction and feedback, applied from external entities. This is less efficient, and often results in a lack of investment/engagement—ultimately impacting morale. Demotivated employees tend to generate a positive feedback loop, spreading the despair throughout the company and then affecting customers, eventually collapsing the entire system.

4. The rules of the system (incentives, punishments, constraints): Rules enable a self-organized group (aided by goals) to work towards that goal. Incentives for attaining or moving towards goals, punishments for the opposite, and implementing and removing system constraints as necessary. Rules change behavior. If a goal is "increase throughput by a factor of two", a self-organizing team can identify constraints (using information flows and feedback loops) and remove them. If the reward for that is a pile of thankless, miserable work, the team be will be unmotivated to accomplish that goal. If a goal is "Watch thirty hours of grueling training videos and take a five hour essay test on the contents" and the punishment for not doing so is losing your job, the team will be motivated to accomplish the goal. If the constraint is "Watch thirty hours of grueling training videos TWICE in one week", it doesn't much matter what the reward or punishment is, chances are it isn't going to happen (or, if it does, the employee will likely not voluntarily remain an employee for long). Scrum wants the sprint demo to be a celebration of work done. This demo should serve as a reward ("you guys did a great job!"). The team internally congratulates each other for a job well done through an iteration, and the company as a whole appreciates its components when the goal is met (or we move towards the goal). Information flow is necessary to identify desired/undesired behavior and constraints. A self-organizing team can use that information (often during the retrospective, though certainly not limited to the retrospective) to create feedback loops to continually ensure they are making good decisions and altering the system to reduce bottlenecks or introduce appropriately-sized buffers.

5. Information flows: Information is used to construct feedback loops. If information is not flowing from, say, "customer satisfaction surveys" to the self-organizing teams, then that information cannot drive the team to take steps to correct or reinforce the changes. If the information is available, the team can apply that information. Without a flow of information, you cannot have feedback loops. Information like this can be identified (e.g. during stand-ups) and inspected (e.g. during a retrospective).6. Driving positive feedback loops.

A positive feedback loop is used to drive change. A measurable goal, for instance, could be used to determine "whatever we last changed got us much closer to the goal, let's adjust what we changed even more!" A positive feedback loop incites more change. An unchecked positive feedback loop will eventually devolve into chaos. If we take the example of a change getting us closer to the goal, the positive feedback loop tells us to make more adjustments, and then more adjustments, and then more adjustments. Imagine these adjustments as changes to the code. A positive feedback loop is telling us to change the code more and more and more, faster and faster and faster. The code-base becomes unstable. The positive feedback loop needs to be controlled (viz. an intentional "slow down!" effort, or a negative feedback loop of unit tests, etc. keeps the rate of change to a controlled level). A retrospective might be used to identify an unchecked positive feedback loop and find a way to control it (e.g. lower the work-in-process).

7. Regulating negative feedback loops: A negative feedback loop is used to ensure stability. The simplest example here is a suite of tests: change too much and the tests will tell you if you the system is becoming unstable/chaotic. On a broader scale, if the goal is measurable and the flow of that measured information is available, a team can determine when it is moving *away* from the goal and take steps to get back on track. A positive feedback loop is a more powerful means to alter a system, but can suffer without negative feedback loops. The flow of information is necessary before a positive feedback loop can be successful, and the flow of information must be able to inform decisions about an unchecked positive feedback loop. The flow of information can (should?) be used to implement a negative feedback loop, but must at least be capable of informing a team that manual intervention of a positive feedback loop is necessary. The size of the loop is critical: too small of a loop and the constant changing of directions will become chaotic (imagine a unit test that runs on every keystroke and informs the entire team of the output); too large of a loop and the scope of change will become difficult to accommodate and result in over- and under-shooting the target (imagine a suite of tests that run once per week. Tests fail, and everybody tries to correct the behavior, ultimately undoing most of the work that was done, even though that wasn't necessary to correct the instability).

8. Material stocks and flows: Stock is inventory/buffer. In lean manufacturing applied to software development, the raw material would be product backlog items that have not yet been refined. The buffer would be processed backlog items (ready to be worked). And the queue of "done" work items waiting for release is the "finished inventory". All three of these stocks are, in lean software principles, waste. You only want as much inventory as you absolutely need because inventory incurs a "carrying cost"—unprocessed backlog items must be processed before they can be worked, and must be tracked by product management. If those items will never be used, we're wasting effort in simply having them. Processed backlog items that won't be used are even more of a waste, since time has been spent preparing them to be ready to work (this is why you only want to groom enough work for a couple of sprints). And finished inventory is wasteful, (a) because it isn't earning you any money even though the work has been done, and (b) because what if we got it wrong? Our feedback loop is too long, we're waiting until the last possible moment to be able to correct our approach, and we will need to rework more than we should if we got dependent work wrong as a result of getting the precursory work wrong. The larger the buffer (raw or processed material), the more leeway you have to mess with the constraints down the line; if you increase capacity of a subsequent operation (e.g. flow of development), you run the danger of running a small buffer down to nothing. If the buffer is empty, the raw materials coming in must exceed the usual capacity in order to restore the buffer, which (in our situation, with external market needs driving the majority of the backlog), turns the market into a bottleneck. This carries the danger of affecting flow—making every subsequent operation in our cycle idle (nothing to do, running at well below desired capacity, ultimately affecting sales [nothing new to sell]).

9. Numbers: The least effective way to alter a system lies in the direct and uninformed adjustment of numbers in that system. With all of the above systems in place, altering the numbers is an informed process (with feedback loops) carried out according to rules put in place by a self-organizing team operating under a measurable goal (driven by a common mindset). Without the above systems, adjusting a number is simply a blind guess. We need the ability to adjust buffers and inventory. We need the ability to assign value to a proposed unit of work. We need to identify constraints to improve throughput (flow) in order to increase sales or customer retention. We need to know when we're understaffed or when manual processes should become automated processes. Making those changes involves changing numbers (e.g. number of resources, numbers of items to work in a sprint, acceptable margins on work performed), but making those changes without focusing on the other places of leverage in a system are unlikely to succeed.
